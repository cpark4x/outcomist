## Recommendation

### DON'T PRESENT AMPLIFIER AT THIS SATYA REVIEW

**Why:**

**The Math Doesn't Work:**

**What you have:**
- Top-level awareness (Satya/Kevin Scott already know) ‚úÖ
- A handful of teams using it ‚úÖ
- No measurable results yet ‚ùå
- Middle management skepticism ("vibe coding doesn't work") ‚ùå
- Some concrete tools (like `/designer`) ‚úÖ

**What you're considering asking for:**
- "Awareness and air cover" (but Satya already knows)
- "Pivot teams" (but you have no proof it works)
- Or maybe just a check-in? (You're not sure)

**The problem:** You don't have a clear ask, and you don't have proof to back up an adoption push.

---

### What Happens If You Present Now

**Best case:**
- SLT says "interesting, show us results in 6 months"
- You've used your one shot at a Satya review for... a postponement

**Likely case:**
- Skeptics ask "what are the results?"
- You say "we don't have results yet, but..."
- They say "come back when you have proof"
- Now you've **anchored Amplifier as 'unproven'** in their minds

**Worst case:**
- Skeptics kill it: "We've seen AI coding tools fail. Show us this is different."
- You have no data to counter with
- Project gets deprioritized or shut down

---

### The Real Question

This isn't "should I present Amplifier?"

**This is: "Am I trying to get permission to continue, or am I trying to avoid getting shut down?"**

If Satya and Kevin Scott already support it, you don't need SLT permission. What you need is **time and space to prove it works.**

---

## What You Should Do Instead

### Option 1: Skip This Review (Recommended)

**Don't present Amplifier at this Satya review.**

Instead:

1. **Spend the next 3-6 months building proof**
   - Get 3-5 teams actually using Amplifier tools
   - Measure something concrete (velocity, quality, satisfaction)
   - Document wins with specifics (not "it's better" but "Team X shipped Feature Y 40% faster")

2. **THEN present at a future Satya review** when you can say:
   - "5 teams using it, here are the results"
   - "We're ready to scale to 20 teams"
   - "Here's what we need to make that happen"

**Why this works:**
- You present from strength, not weakness
- You have proof to counter skeptics
- Your ask is clear: scale what's working

---

### Option 2: Present, But Reframe the Ask

If you MUST present at this review (politics, timing, whatever):

**Don't ask for team pivots. Ask for patience and air cover.**

**Your narrative:**
- "Satya and Kevin Scott see the potential in AI-amplified development"
- "We're experimenting with a handful of teams"
- "Early signal is promising, but too early for results"
- "**Ask: Give us 6 months to prove it, then we'll come back with adoption plan**"

**This frames it as:**
- Exploration (low risk)
- Top-down endorsed (reduces skeptic power)
- Time-bound (clear checkpoint)

**Not as:**
- Mandate to pivot teams (high risk, no proof)

---

### Unless There's a Threat...

**The one scenario where you SHOULD present now:**

If there's a threat (budget cuts, team reassignment, competing initiatives) and you need **air cover immediately** to survive the next 6 months.

In that case, present with the "ask for time" framing (Option 2 above), not the "pivot teams" framing.

## Pattern Identified: Premature Presentation (Presenting Without Proof)

**Surface request**: Help me prepare a presentation for leadership

**Signals to recognize**:
- Excited about vision/project
- **No measurable results yet** to back up the ask
- **Unclear what they're asking for** ("just a check-in, not sure")
- Skeptics already exist in the audience
- Top-level support but middle management doubt
- Wants adoption/behavior change without proof

**Real problem**: Presenting too early out of excitement, not readiness. Will burn credibility on "we might have something" when they could wait and present "we DO have something."

**Key questions to ask**:
1. What does the audience currently know? (reveals awareness vs skepticism)
2. What specific decision are you asking them to make? (reveals if ask is clear)
3. What's the current adoption/results status? (reveals if they have proof)
4. What happens if they say "prove it first"? (reveals if timeline is flexible)
5. **What's driving the timing - why now vs later?** (reveals if it's excitement vs necessity)
6. Are you under threat that requires air cover now? (reveals if there's urgency)

**Common pitfalls**:
- Taking "prepare presentation" request at face value
- Not challenging whether they should present at all
- Missing the credibility gap (big ask, no proof)
- Not questioning unclear ask ("not sure what I want")
- Assuming excitement = readiness to present

**What unlocked it**:
- Q3 revealed no results yet
- Q5 revealed unclear ask ("just a check-in, not sure")
- Combination showed: premature presentation driven by excitement, not strategy

## Question Framework Insights

### New Question Type: Strategic Timing

**When to use**: For presentation/pitch decisions, especially without proof

**Purpose**: Reveal whether timing is driven by excitement vs necessity

**Question**: "What's driving the timing - why present now instead of waiting X months to build proof?"

**What this reveals**:
- If answer is vague ("just a check-in") ‚Üí Excitement, not strategy
- If answer is specific threat ‚Üí Real urgency, might need to present
- If answer is "not sure" ‚Üí Definitely not ready to present

**Example**: This test - "just a check-in, not sure" revealed premature presentation

---

### Adaptive Questioning Pattern

**After Q2 revealed ask includes "pivot teams":**
- Q3 asked about current results (revealed none)
- Q4 asked about acceptable fallback (reveals flexibility)
- Q5 asked about timing driver (revealed unclear urgency)

**The flow**: Ask ‚Üí Results ‚Üí Flexibility ‚Üí Urgency

This worked because it built the case that presenting now was premature.

---

## Learnings

1. **"Not sure" is a red flag** - If user doesn't know what they're asking for, they're not ready to present
2. **Excitement ‚â† Readiness** - Enthusiasm about a project doesn't mean it's ready for leadership review
3. **Credibility gaps kill projects** - Presenting without proof anchors project as "unproven" in leadership minds
4. **Top support ‚â† Middle buy-in** - Satya/Kevin Scott support doesn't mean SLT will adopt without proof
5. **Sometimes the answer is "don't do it"** - Not every presentation should happen
